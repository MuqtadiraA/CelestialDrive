1934–1937 Chrysler Airflow

Restored Models: $45,000 – $110,000 USD

The Chrysler Airflow, produced from 1934 to 1937, was a groundbreaking vehicle that revolutionized automotive design. It was the first American production car to be designed using aerodynamic principles and wind-tunnel testing, making it a true pioneer in automotive history. Although it wasn’t commercially successful at the time, its innovative design influenced future cars and is now considered one of the most iconic pre-war cars.

Design & Exterior

The Chrysler Airflow was designed with a focus on aerodynamics and modernity. Key exterior features include:

  • Streamlined, rounded grille integrated with the hood
  • Flush-mounted headlights
  • Curved, all-steel body panels
  • Forward-cab design for improved aerodynamics
  • Rear wheel fender skirts (some models)
  • Chrome detailing with horizontal trim lines

Interior Features

The Chrysler Airflow's interior was equally impressive, with a focus on luxury, comfort, and space:

  • Seating for 5–7 passengers
  • Materials like mohair, wool broadcloth, and chrome trim
  • Reclining rear seats (optional)
  • Extra legroom due to the innovative cab-forward layout
  • Rear-seat armrests and folding center armrest
  • Improved soundproofing and insulation for a quieter ride

Powertrain & Performance

The Chrysler Airflow offered solid performance for its time, with a range of engine options:

  • Engine Options:
    • 4.9L Inline-8 (Chrysler Series)
    • 5.3L Inline-8 (Chrysler Imperial models)
  • Horsepower: 115 – 130 hp
  • Transmission: 3-speed manual with synchromesh
  • Top Speed: ~95 mph
  • Fuel Economy: ~14–16 mpg
  • Suspension: Independent coil spring (front), live axle with leaf springs (rear)

Innovation & Technology

The Chrysler Airflow was a pioneer in automotive design, introducing several innovations that would shape the future:

  • First U.S. car designed with wind-tunnel testing
  • Early adopter of unit-body construction
  • Engine placed over front axle for better weight distribution
  • Improved crash safety with a reinforced body
  • Enhanced ride comfort with a lower center of gravity

Cultural & Market Impact

The Chrysler Airflow was a critical success but a commercial failure, as its futuristic design was too radical for many 1930s consumers:

  • Initial Reception: Critically praised but commercially unsuccessful
  • Public Resistance: Too radical and futuristic for many at the time
  • Legacy: Influenced future designs like the Lincoln Zephyr and Tucker 48
  • Now a sought-after collectible among pre-war car collectors

Pricing & Market Value

The Chrysler Airflow has seen its market value increase significantly over time:

  • Original MSRP (1934–1937): $1,345 – $1,695 USD
  • Restored Models: $45,000 – $110,000 USD
  • Concours-Level Condition: $125,000+
  • Limousine & Imperial Models: Higher premiums due to rarity
